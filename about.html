About

Curating the hours

The Earthly Book of Hours — Horae Terrenae in Latin — is a daily primer of literature, art, and ecology; a contemplative archive of images, readings, and natural phenomena for an uncertain age. It's neither a mystical text nor a self-help manual, but perhaps something in between—a portiforium poeticum or perhaps a modernist breviary for encountering the day (or night) and yourself in it. It's a collection of texts, images, and phenomena for observing biological and cultural rhythms, seeing inside or under or through. Use it to navigate the nychthemeron and chart your chronotype.

The project draws formal inspiration from earlier books of hours, the illuminated manuscripts of medieval Europe that organized daily prayer around the eight canonical hours (matins, vespers, etc.) and often included calendars, seasonal illustrations, and a variety of short readings. My contemporary adaptation of the form offers a poetic rendition of the original hours, dividing the day into eight three-hour blocks meant to evoke to the natural, psychological, and sociological rhythms of the day:

  • Void (midnight through 2:00 a.m.)
  • Hush (3:00 a.m. through 5:00 a.m.)
  • Chorus (6:00 a.m. through 8:00 a.m.)
  • Transit (9:00 a.m. through 11:00 a.m.)
  • Fulcrum (noon through 2:00 p.m.)
  • Doldrums (3:00 p.m. through 5:00 p.m.)
  • Convivium (6:00 p.m. through 8:00 p.m.)
  • Denouement (9:00 p.m. through 11:00 p.m.)

Each block includes four elements:

  • (1) a selection of artworks, diagrams, and other images;
  • (2) a collection of readings drawn from literature, philosophy, and other domains;
  • (3) a list of natural phenomena occuring around that time; and
  • (4) a brief poetic statement, or versicle.

When you visit, the site displays content from the current hour. As the day changes around you, so does the site's content — a gentle reminder that time is always moving, and that each section of the day has its own particular, if unpredictably varying, character.

The Sources page will (eventually) provide full documentation of the reading and images included in the hours, along with links to freely available texts, contextual essays, and further reading.

The texts and images gathered here are drawn primarily from the public domain, representing works whose passage into common inheritance makes this kind of curation possible. Where copyright restrictions apply to works included here, it does so in the spirit of fair use: for educational, illustrative, and non-commercial purposes, in the belief that brief encounter leads to deeper reading. Some links to books and editions mentioned in the hours are provided through Bookshop.org; any small commission supports independent booksellers and helps sustain this project. If you are a rights holder with a concern about a specific work, please be in touch.

This site is an ongoing project. If you have suggestions for quotes, images, or natural phenomena to include, or if you'd like to follow along with this work and related activities, subscribe to Lectio Terra on Substack.
