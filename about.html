About

Curating the hours

The Earthly Book of Hours — Horae Terrenae in Latin — is a daily primer of literature, art, and ecology: a portiforium poeticum or perhaps a modernist breviary for encountering the day (or night) and yourself in it. It's a collection of texts, images, and phenomena for observing biological and cultural rhythms, seeing inside or under or through or beyond. Use it to navigate the nychthemeron and chart your diel cycles.

Horae Terrenae — The Earthly Book of Hours — is a meditation on time, nature, and the cyclical patterns that structure our days. It maps the 24 hours of the day to eight poetic "blocks," each associated with a particular quality of light, a cast of natural phenomena, and a collection of readings from literature, philosophy, and natural history.

The project draws formal inspiration from earlier books of hours, the illuminated manuscripts of medieval Europe that organized daily prayer around the eight canonical hours (matins, vespers, etc.) and often included calendars, seasonal illustrations, and a variety of short readings. This contemporary adaptation of the form offers a poetic rendition of the original hours, dividing the day into eight three-hour blocks attuned to the natural, physical, and sociological rhythms of the day.

Each block includes four elements:

(1) a selection of artworks, diagrams, and other images;
(2) a collection of readings drawn from literature, philosophy, and other domains;
(3) a list of natural phenomena occuring around that time; and
(4) a brief poetic statement, or versicle.

When you visit, the site displays content from the current hour. As the day changes around you, so does the content — a gentle reminder that time is always moving, and that each hour has its own particular character.

The Sources page will (eventually) provide full documentation of the reading and images included in the hours, along with links to freely available texts, contextual information, and recommended editions.

The texts and images gathered here are drawn primarily from the public domain, representing works whose passage into common inheritance makes this kind of curation possible. Where copyrighted material appears, it does so in the spirit of fair use: for educational, illustrative, and non-commercial purposes, in the belief that brief encounter leads to deeper reading. Some links to books and editions are affiliate links through Bookshop.org; any small commission supports independent booksellers and helps sustain this project. If you are a rights holder with a concern about a specific work, please be in touch.

This site is an ongoing project. If you have suggestions for quotes, images, or natural phenomena to include, or if you'd like to follow along with this work and related activities, subscribe to Lectio Terra on Substack.
